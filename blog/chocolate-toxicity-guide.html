What To Do If Your Dog Eats Chocolate: A Step-by-Step Emergency Guide

Published: April 27, 2026 • 5 min read • Emergency Care

Finding a torn chocolate wrapper on the floor while your dog licks their chops is a pet parent's worst nightmare. Chocolate toxicity is one of the most common reasons for emergency vet visits. But before you panic, it's essential to understand exactly what makes chocolate dangerous and how to evaluate the risk.

Why is Chocolate Toxic to Dogs?

Chocolate contains two compounds that are highly toxic to dogs: theobromine and caffeine (both classified as methylxanthines). While humans can metabolize these compounds quickly, dogs process them very slowly. This allows the toxins to build up in their system, leading to dangerous physical reactions.

The 3 Crucial Factors

The severity of chocolate poisoning depends entirely on three factors:

  • Your Dog's Weight: A large Labrador can handle a piece of chocolate that might be fatal to a Chihuahua.
  • The Type of Chocolate: Baking chocolate and cocoa powder are the most dangerous. White chocolate contains almost zero theobromine.
  • The Amount Eaten: The dosage determines the poison.

Common Symptoms of Chocolate Poisoning

Symptoms usually appear within 6 to 12 hours after ingestion. Look out for:

  • Vomiting and diarrhea
  • Increased thirst and urination
  • Restlessness and panting
  • Elevated heart rate
  • In severe cases: muscle tremors, seizures, and heart failure

What Will the Vet Do?

If the toxicity calculator indicates a moderate to high risk, or if your dog is showing symptoms, get to an emergency vet immediately. Treatment may include inducing vomiting (if caught early), administering activated charcoal to block absorption, and providing IV fluids to help flush the toxins out of their system.
